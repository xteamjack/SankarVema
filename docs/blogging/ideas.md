## Pillar 1 — The AI-Native Enterprise *(rethinking the firm itself)*

**Thesis:** *You can't bolt AI onto an org designed for humans and get AI-native economics. Most "AI transformations" are org-preservation projects wearing a transformation costume.*

Hard questions (each is a post):

- If you rebuilt your company from scratch today, knowing what AI can do — would it have this org chart? Then **why does it?**
- Coase said firms exist because coordination is expensive. AI collapses coordination cost. **So what is the firm even *for* now?**
- Where does judgment live when execution is automated? (The middle is thinning — you're automating the layer that *made* your seniors.)
- Are you automating tasks, or redesigning the process? Most "AI initiatives" pave the cowpath — same broken process, now faster.
- What's your unit of work — the person, the team, or the agent? Your operating model assumes one answer; your next three years need a different one.

---

## Pillar 2 — Where the Value Actually Goes *(value & capital allocation)*

**Thesis:** *Most enterprise AI spend produces demos, not P&L — and the value doesn't accrue where the CFO thinks it does.*

Hard questions:

- If every competitor gets the *same* productivity lift from the *same* models — is that a strategy or a treadmill? **Where did your advantage go?**
- Are you building a capability you *own*, or renting one whose price the model layer controls? Who captures the margin in three years — you, or your vendor?
- Efficiency is table stakes everyone reaches. Advantage is proprietary data + workflow + judgment. **Which one are you actually funding?**
- What's your AI capital thesis — cost-out, top-line, or new business model? Most boards fund cost-out and act surprised when it commoditizes.
- The compounding value is in year two — the platform, the data flywheel, the eval loop. Are you funding a quarter and expecting a decade?

## Pillar 3 — The Human Question *(talent, cost, and "the spiral")*

**Thesis:** *I came up through the industry built on human-cost arbitrage. AI is the next wave — it rhymes, but it doesn't repeat. Leaders who treat it as pure cost-out win the quarter and lose the decade.*

This is the emotional and moral center — and the most credible pillar. Hard questions:

- If you automate every junior role, **where do your future seniors come from?** You're eating your own apprenticeship pipeline for a margin point.
- Cost-out is *symmetric* — every competitor does it too. So what did cutting 40% of headcount actually *buy* you, competitively?
- Institutional knowledge lives in the people you're about to let go. Is it captured — or are you lobotomizing the firm and calling it efficiency?
- What do you owe the people whose roles you automate? And is that only a moral question, or also a talent-brand and trust question that shows up in your ability to hire the *next* ones?
- Reskilling: real budget line, or the sentence you say on the earnings call?

### The Spiral — flagship essay (or 3-part series)

The anchor piece of the whole platform. The argument spine:

> AI cuts headcount → margin rises → the market rewards it → competitors match within a quarter → the margin advantage competes away → now *everyone* has lower cost **and** a shrinking base of employed customers → pressure to cut deeper → but you've automated the juniors, so no new seniors get made → judgment atrophies → you grow dependent on a model vendor whose price you don't set → and the margin you "saved" gets captured by the intelligence layer, not you.

That's the spiral. Real, non-obvious, and genuinely hard — which is why CxOs will share it: it articulates the thing they feel but can't name.

One-line authority to write it: *"I spent twenty years inside the industry that ran on labor arbitrage. I've watched a cost-transformation spiral before. Here's what AI's version rhymes with — and the one place it breaks the pattern."*

**Do not end in doom.** End on the way out: the firms that treat AI as **leverage on human judgment** rather than a **substitute for human cost** are the ones that escape the spiral. That's the advisory thesis actually being sold.

## Pillar 4 — Competing When Intelligence Is Cheap *(strategy & disruption)*

**Thesis:** *AI doesn't give you a moat. It dissolves everyone's moats at once — including yours. The real question isn't "how do I adopt AI," it's "what's still scarce when intelligence is a utility?"*

Hard questions:

- When intelligence becomes a utility, **what's still scarce?** (Proprietary data, distribution, trust, regulatory access, real-world assets.) That's where your strategy has to migrate.
- Your moat *was* scale, process, and expertise. AI commoditizes process and expertise. **What's left?**
- Fast-follower used to be the safe bet. Is it, when the leader's data flywheel compounds *daily*?
- Are you disrupting yourself, or waiting to be disrupted by someone who has nothing to protect?
- Build vs. buy isn't a procurement decision — it's "where do I want to own the compounding asset." Most enterprises rent the one thing they should own.

## How to run it

- **Depth over frequency.** Not 3-posts-a-week builder content. One genuinely hard, well-argued piece a week (or fortnightly) out-performs daily takes with this audience. CxOs reward the essay, not the hot take.
- **One hard question per post.** The questions above *are* the hooks — lead with the question, reason about it honestly (including where you're unsure), close with a position.
- **The flywheel:** LinkedIn post poses the question and gives the take → links to the full essay on the site → the essay proves the whole argument holds. "The Spiral" is the anchor everything points back to.
- **Stance discipline:** never hype, never doom. Every post should leave the reader thinking *"this person has actually been in the room where this decision gets made."*
