## Principles I keep coming back to

### 1. Architecture is a series of bets, not a diagram

A diagram tells you what *fits*. A bet tells you what *might break*. Senior
architects spend less time drawing and more time naming the bets out loud:
the failure mode, the latency assumption, the cost curve, the regret
window. The diagram is a compression of the conversation. Don't ship without
the conversation.

### 2. AI projects fail in operations, not in models

Most enterprises hire data scientists when they need platform engineers, MLOps
people, and a feedback loop. The model is the easy part. The hard parts are
data pipelines, eval, monitoring, retraining, rollback, governance, and the
org change to actually consume the output. Architect for the second year.

### 3. Open source over patents

I've held this view for a long time. Compounding wins are upstream, not behind
a fence. Where I'm useful is in the design and the judgment, not in the
hoarding.

### 4. Build for the team you'll have, not the team you have

The org you'll need in 12 months looks nothing like today's. Hire and architect
ahead of growth, not behind it. The single most expensive mistake I see is
optimizing for the current team and inheriting their constraints.

### 5. Ship the thinnest end-to-end first

A pipeline that works on one example end-to-end teaches you more than
a perfect component that doesn't connect to anything. This is true at the model
level, the system level, and the org level.

## How I prefer to work

- **Deep over wide**: I do my best work when I'm the architect-on-call for a
  small number of serious engagements, not advising forty companies at arm's
  length.
- **Written before spoken**: I'd rather read a one-pager than sit through a
  forty-minute deck. Whatever we discuss, expect me to write back.
- **Decisions over status**: Status updates are inventory. Decisions are
  velocity. Bias toward the latter.
