Most enterprise AI programs do not fail at the model. They fail in the second year.

After two decades inside enterprise transformation, the pattern is remarkably consistent, and almost none of it is about the model.

Enterprise AI succeeds when three things are present at the same time: a real problem with a measurable outcome, a small platform team that owns the AI substrate, and a feedback loop tying production output back to model improvement. Most programs get the first wrong, skip the second, and never build the third. Then they restart with a different model.

THE FIVE-LAYER REALITY CHECK

Problem: a real problem, or a board-deck problem? Measurable in dollars, hours or risk, not "efficiency."

Data: where the program actually dies. If the data is not ready, no model recovers from it.

Platform: a small team (5 to 10 people) owning evals, the model gateway, observability, governance and the prompt registry. Without it, every team builds its own stack and you end up with 14 half-working AI projects.

Product: AI inside an existing workflow beats a new AI tool. Stand up a separate UX and adoption collapses.

Feedback: production output, to eval, to the next version of the model or prompt. Skip it and the system quietly gets worse as the world changes around it.

THE ORG DESIGN THAT WORKS

A central, small AI platform team. Embedded AI engineers per product line who consume the platform. Domain experts paired with engineers, not sitting on advisory committees. A governance function with veto power.

What does not work: a Center of Excellence that owns no product. That is a budget line, not a structure.

WHAT CXOS UNDERESTIMATE

The unglamorous work, data, evals, observability, governance, is 60 percent of the budget. AI engineers want to work on AI, not data pipelines, so architect around that or lose them. And the second year creates more value than the first, but only if the platform layer exists.

The programs that compound are boring on purpose. The exciting ones restart every year.

So before the next model debate: do you have the problem, the platform and the feedback loop, or just the model?
