## The mandate

As a nodal officer with the Ministry of Shipping, Government of India, I led digital
adoption across major Indian port trusts including Visakhapatnam, Chennai and Jawaharlal
Nehru (JNPT). This was my first leadership posting, and it was at the scale of national
infrastructure, with progress reported quarterly to the Prime Minister's Office.

## The problem

A nation's ports are its trade choke points. When port operations run on paper, the cost is
not local inefficiency, it is friction on the whole economy: slow clearance, poor visibility,
and no common language between the many parties a single shipment touches. Digitalizing a port
is not installing software, it is coordinating operators, customs, shipping lines and freight
forwarders around new systems and shared standards, under public accountability.

## The work

- **Port operations systems.** Oversaw implementation of terminal operating systems, gate
  management and yard management across the port trusts, working closely with the vendors who
  built them.
- **Standards, not just software.** Served on the committee designing EDIFACT-based standards
  for shipping and cargo operations in India, so systems across ports could actually talk to
  each other.
- **Many stakeholders, one system.** Coordinated port officials, customs, shipping lines and
  freight forwarders, the constituency that has to agree for a digital port to work.
- **Public accountability.** Submitted quarterly progress on port digitalization directly to
  the Prime Minister's Office.

## What it proves

Two decades before the AI work, this set the altitude: delivering technology at the scale of a
nation's infrastructure, where the stakeholders are institutions and the accountability runs to
the top of government. It is where I learned that the hardest part of a large system is rarely
the code.
